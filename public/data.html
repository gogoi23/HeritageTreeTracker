FUN FACTS

London Plantree

The London Planetree [on NW/C SW PARK & SW MAIN ST] was planted by Sylvester Farrell in the planting strip in front of his house, as determined by historic photos of the home that show the newly planted tree. This tree has seen its surroundings change several times over the course of its life. Today, the lot is being used as a parking lot. London planetrees are notable for : their resilience to the stressors of the urban environment and their longevity. This heritage tree may well see the land around it continue to change as the city continues to grow and develop.


Beech Tree

The beech tree [on 10115 NE THOMPSON ST] was brought to its current location as a seedling all the way from Missouri in the early 20th century and planted by a Mrs. Moore in what is now the Parkrose Heights neighborhood of Portland. Neighbors petitioned Multnomah County to remove the tree in 1977, but their request was denied. Parkrose Heights was an affluent municipality outside of Portland proper until it was annexed by the City in 1983. Annexation brought about changes in land-use from farms to apartment blocks and higher taxes. Parkrose Heights is one of the most diverse neighborhoods in Portland as a result of having a high population of immigrants and those displaced by the gentrification of close-in neighborhoods.


White Oak

The germination of this heritage Oregon white oak [on 1310-1338 NW 23RD AVE] is thought to predate the establishment of Portland as a city by white settlers in 1843. Oak savannah was the dominant habitat of the Willamette Valley, and it is possible that this white oak is a remnant of the balanced management practices carried out by indigenous Chinookan people on a large scale across the Willamette Valley. Controlled burns were used to maintain oak savannah for promoting growth of food staples such as camas, berries, and acorns and minimizing competition of conifers and shrubs that would compete with Oregon white oaks. Fires shocked oaks into producing mast crops of acorns, which would be preserved and used for food and trade. Today, this oak is adjacent to the sidewalk in NW Portland along a busy thoroughfare lined with shops and restaurants, still thriving in the city grew around it.


American (Burrell) Elm

The Burrell Elm [1111 SW 10th AVE] is the second historic tree recognized by the City of Portland. It is an American Elm tree that was planted in 1870 at the home of Martin and Rosetta Burrell, in what was their private garden at the time. As Portland grew, the character of the neighborhood changed from private single-family homes to denser development. This elm tree is now behind the sidewalk in front of the YWCA in downtown Portland.


Tulip Tree

George Nicolai built a house on this lot for his new wife and planted the tulip tree [on 1403 NE WEIDLER ST] after construction was completed, in the late 1880s. The use of this lot has changed over the decades since the tree was planted, resulting in some construction damage to the roots. However, this tree thrives along busy NE 15th Ave.
